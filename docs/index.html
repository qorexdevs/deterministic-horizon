The economics: cost per correct solution

"Think harder" isn't free — deeper chains burn more tokens and succeed less often, so the cost per correct answer explodes past the horizon. A tool pays a flat call cost at near-constant accuracy. Two theories, four falsifiable predictions

Prior work blames a trained Simplicity Bias (a preference fine-tuning should fix). This paper argues for an architectural Decoherence limit (a wall fine-tuning can't move). They disagree on four measurable predictions — and the data backs the architectural one every time.

The theory in three steps

No magic — just three equations. Everything in the explorer above is computed from exactly these, matching src/policy.py line for line.

1

Per-step error grows with position (Definition 4.1 / Theorem 4.2)

Each reasoning step isn't equally reliable. The error rate climbs the deeper you are in the chain, because attention's usable state resolution degrades — not at the raw context length, but at the far shorter effective decoherence length L_eff.

ε(d)= ε0+γ· dLeff
2

Integrate it → super-exponential decay (Theorem 4.2)

Multiplying the per-step success probabilities along the chain gives a closed form. The quadratic term in the exponent is the "wall": accuracy doesn't fade linearly, it collapses.

P(correct at depth d) ≈exp( −d·ε0− γ·d(d+1) 2·Leff )
3

Solve P(correct)=α for the horizon (Theorem 4.5)

Set the decay equal to your success threshold α and solve the quadratic. The positive root is the Deterministic Horizon — a single, usable engineering number. For GPT-4o (ε₀=0.02, γ=0.15, L_eff=150, α=0.5) it lands at d*≈22.3.

d∗= −ε0·Leff+ ε02· Leff2+ 2γ·Leff· ln(1/α) γ
📐

Attention Bottleneck (Thm 4.4)

Trackable states are capped by architecture: |S| ≤ c·2^(H·log₂(L/H)·√d_h). Capacity, not training.

🔒

Fine-tuning ceiling (Thm 4.7)

Recovery is bounded by O(d*/d). Predicts <5% gain; the data shows +3.2%. The wall holds.

🧭

The takeaway

Below d*, reason. Above d*, delegate. Knowing the boundary is the whole contribution.
